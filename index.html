Waste Colonialism, Explained.

In 2015, the U.S. exported an astounding 4.5 billion pounds of scrap plastic to other nations.

Thankfully, U.S. plastic exports have declined significantly from 2015 to the present, largely
due to China’s 2017 plastic import ban. Scroll down to explore a visual representation
of this dramatic shift. You'll notice a sharp drop in U.S. exports between 2017 and 2019,
reflecting the immediate impact of China’s policy change.


However, the decline in U.S. plastic exports is only part of the equation.

Like all climate challenges, addressing waste exports requires global coordination
—yet some countries remain uncooperative. The following graph illustrates how EU plastic
exports to Malaysia have not followed the same downward trend as the U.S. In fact, in
recent years, EU exports of plastic waste have been steadily increasing.

In 2023, the EU exported 595,248,108 pounds of plastic.




One average adult elephant weighs 12,000 pounds.



This means in 2023, the EU exported ~50,000 elephants worth of plastic waste.
A whopping 47% of it went to Malaysia.

As a result of China’s plastic import ban, plastic waste from the Global North is disproportionately exported to other countries in the Global South.

Malaysia became a leading destination for plastic waste after the ban.

These countries are forced to bear the grave health and environmental consequences that come with the Global North's plastic waste.

"Every night they burn. I see black smoke at night, so I go over and ask him 'why are you trying to kill me?' They ignore me," said one Malaysian worker in the Pulau Indah industrial zone, who did not want to be identified.

Waste colonialism persists as a problem globally. While waste goes out of sight in the Global North, communities living in the Global South suffer from its consequences.

After plastic waste from the Global North lands in the Global South, it has to be burned.

Factories in the Global South engage in open burning plastics, releasing toxic chemicals into the atmosphere and causing air pollution, significantly impacting both the public health and environment of its local people.

When plastic waste ends up in landfills, it contaminates the surrounding soil and water sources. Consequently, local ecosystems suffer. This also means that the food and water consumed by locals contain toxic chemicals.

As long as waste colonialism exists, marginalized communities globally must live through realities where their environments are degrading, air is being polluted, health is being compromised — a reality that is forced upon them by richer nations.
